Shared sync

By default Krama saves in this browser only. Connect a free Supabase project and the same plan appears on your phone, your laptop and your father's device — everyone editing the same live data.

  1. Create a free project at supabase.com.
  2. In SQL Editor, run the contents of supabase-schema.sql from this repo.
  3. In Settings → API, copy the Project URL and the anon public key.
  4. Paste them below and connect — on every device that should see the plan.

Read this before you put real plans in it. With only the anon key, anyone who gets the URL and key can read and edit the plan. That is fine while it is just you and your father on your own devices. Before this goes on a public subdomain it needs Supabase Auth with row-level security, so only signed-in accounts can touch the data.

How Krama works — and what Agile actually means

The one-paragraph version

Agile came from software teams who were tired of planning a year ahead and discovering at the end that the plan was wrong. The whole idea is small: work in short fixed cycles, finish something real in each one, then look at what happened and adjust. Everything else is vocabulary.

The rhythm — four moments, every two weeks

  1. Planning (Monday, ~1 hour, together). Look at capacity. Pull tasks from the Backlog into the sprint until committed hours reach capacity — and then stop. Write one sentence as the sprint goal.
  2. Daily check (5 minutes, alone). Open Today. What did I finish, what am I on, what is stuck. Move a card. That is all.
  3. Review (end of sprint, together). Open the Review tab and show the finished work — a demo, not a status report. Anything not finished goes back to the backlog.
  4. Retrospective (same sitting, 15 minutes). Not what got done — how it went. One thing to change next sprint. Write it down in the Review tab.

Being honest about the parts that will not work

Most of Scrum is machinery for coordinating six to nine people. Working alone, three things carry nearly all the value and the rest is ceremony:

  • The fixed boundary. Every two weeks you are forced to stop and re-decide instead of drifting between forty-five projects.
  • Measured velocity. After three sprints you know your real capacity. This is the antidote to optimistic planning, which is what actually kills solo plans.
  • A scheduled review with someone else. Knowing you will have to show finished work to your father on a fixed date changes what you do on the days in between. This is the part that makes it work.

Two rules worth keeping

  • Do not change the sprint mid-sprint. New ideas go to the backlog, not into this week. If something genuinely cannot wait, take something else out — capacity is fixed.
  • Meetings are not tasks. Add them as commitments. They come out of capacity before you plan, so you plan against hours you actually have.

For Papa

You can edit anything here. In Agile terms the useful role is not manager but stakeholder: you set what matters most, you receive the demo, and you hold the deadline. The most valuable thing you can do is comment on tasks — every comment shows up in the Review tab, so nothing said gets lost between sittings.
